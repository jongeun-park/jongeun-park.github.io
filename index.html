Job Market Paper

"Environmental Enforcement during Legislative Vacancies: Insights from Clean Air Act Inspections'',
with Abdoul G. Sam [Abstract]

Congressional members may seek to influence the enforcement of environmental regulations to enhance their re-election prospects. When congressional seats become vacant, primarily due to resignation or death, regulators may experience temporary independence from such political influences. We explore the effects of congressional vacancies on Clean Air Act inspections. We find vacancies of Democratic congressional members reduce inspection probability by 20.4% and inspection frequency by 25% compared to Democratic incumbency. This suggests regulators might increase inspections to align with Democratic members' preferences and revert to their preferred levels once these members leave Congress. We also find Republican vacancies lead to an increase in inspections for significant violators of Clean Air Act, compared to Republican incumbency, indicating incumbent Republicans may suppress inspections for these facilities. Our findings shed light on how political actors affect environmental enforcement and how regulators respond to legislative vacancies, offering insights for improving environmental regulatory effectiveness.

Working Papers

"From Waste to Energy: Assessing the Efficacy of the Landfill Methane Outreach Program", with Abdoul G. Sam [Abstract]

We examine the efficacy of the Landfill Methane Outreach Program (LMOP) in mitigating methane emissions from municipal solid waste landfills in the United States. LMOP is a voluntary program initiated by the United States Environmental Protection Agency to incentivize landfill owners to undertake landfill gas-to-energy projects for the generation of new energy sources. Using a staggered difference-in-differences approach to account for the heterogeneity in the timing of LMOP participation, our empirical results show that the program increases the likelihood of landfill gas-to-energy project development by 25.2%. Despite this positive impact on landfill gas-to-energy project development, no significant reduction in fugitive methane emissions is observed, possibly due to the independent installation of landfill gas collection systems. However, landfill gas-to-energy project development is found to decrease net greenhouse gas emissions—which account for avoided carbon emissions due to energy generation from captured methane gas—by 26.1%. The findings underscore the important contribution of LMOP in promoting landfill gas-to-energy project development and reducing greenhouse gas emissions in the absence of legal mandates.
